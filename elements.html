Covid-19 Risk Factor Descision Tree

With Confusion Matrix to test our accuracy

My goal with this project is to give people the necessary information to make an educated decision based on county risk-level. I decided to take data related to COVID-19 within the United States to develop a Decision Tree for the risk factors of US counties. With the many parameters available to me through CovidActNow.org I was able to specifically select which parameters would play into the risk level of a United States County. My parameters were County, Population, Test Positivity Ratio, Case Density, ICU Capacity, ICU Usage, Infection Rate, and Risk Level(0-5).

Plotting the Decision Tree

In order to isolate these parameters we had to use pandas to manipulate the columns and pick the specific columns that corresponded with the data that we wanted to use. After deleting all the counties that had missing data on my seven parameters, I went from 3222 rows of data to 1585. I then split my data into training and testing data using a 70/30 split with 30% being test data. I was now ready to train my decision tree and opted for the use of gini index. After training our data, I plotted the decision tree to get an idea of the parameters that were used from my dataset. Finally, I tested my model and used a confusion matrix to get an idea of how accurate the tree was classifying my data.

Conclusion of Results

When looking at the decision tree, from the 7 parameters we choose to isolate from the dataset, only 3 were taken into account when deciding the risk factor of a county. case density, test positivity ratio, and infection rate with case density being the root node. As you can see in the figure above, out of the 480 samples, our model was able to correctly predict 474 of the samples at their given risk level.

Support Vector Machines and Decision Regions

With alternating Gamma values to test its affect on the Descision Regions

I trained my data using Support Vector Machine (SVM) with the Radial Basis Function (RBF). Then I split my data randomly. 90% of data as training dataset and 10% of data as test dataset. Then I tested out 5 Different gamma values in RBF (0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1000). For each Gamma value I tested I plotted the graph and its accuracy score. Once all 5 graphs have been created I made a Graph of Accuracy Vs Gamma Values to see what gamma value produces the most accurate grouping.

Gamma = 0.1 Accuracy = 0.817
Gamma = 1 Accuracy = 0.878
Gamma = 10 Accuracy = 0.976
Gamma = 100 Accuracy = 1
